# Investigation Types

Four modes. The investigation type must be identified before any search begins. Different modes require different sources, different diagnostic questions, and different output focus.

---

## Type 1 — Suburb hypothesis

**What it is:** The operator has a suburb or area in mind and wants to know if it supports their concept. They may have a strong feeling about the suburb ("it feels right") or a specific reason for it (demographic fit, cost assumption, observed gap).

**Diagnostic questions to ask first:**
- What's your concept and format specifically?
- What's underneath the hypothesis — is it demographic fit, a rent assumption, an observed gap in the market, or something else?
- Have you walked the strip? What did you observe?
- What have you already looked at or ruled out?

**Why this matters:** "The suburb feels right" is a hypothesis, not a conclusion. The research must test the hypothesis — including looking for the evidence that would disprove it, not just the evidence that supports it.

**Source priority for this mode:**
1. ABS QuickStats — test the demographic assumption
2. CommercialRealEstate.com.au — test the rent assumption
3. PlanningAlerts.org.au — check for approved DAs that would change the character of the area
4. Café density sources (Yelp, Google Maps) — establish the competitive set and test the "gap" hypothesis
5. JLL/CBRE report if available — benchmark the lease market context

**Output focus:** Whether the data supports or conflicts with the stated hypothesis. The conflict section is the most important output for this mode — the operator needs to know where the data disagrees with their read.

---

## Type 2 — Specific listing

**What it is:** The operator has a specific going concern (café for sale) or a specific commercial space in front of them. They want to know what the public data says about it.

**Diagnostic questions to ask first:**
- What information has been provided — full P&L, or just a summary?
- What do you know about the lease — remaining term, rent, review mechanism?
- What's the asking price and what's the stated turnover or rent?
- What do you know about why it's available?

**Why this matters:** The broker pack or listing summary is a sales document. The research job is to find what the document doesn't say — not to evaluate the document on its own terms.

**Source priority for this mode:**
1. Bsale.com.au (for going concerns) — comparable asking prices in the same area and category
2. CommercialRealEstate.com.au — lease rate context for that specific strip
3. PlanningAlerts.org.au — DA pipeline for the specific address and surrounding block
4. Café density sources — understand the competitive environment this listing sits within

**Output focus:** What the asking price or lease rate looks like against comparable market data, what information the listing has not provided that would materially change the picture, and what the DA pipeline reveals about the medium-term environment.

The "what this investigation cannot tell you" section is critical for this mode — the most important information (actual net profit, actual reason for sale, actual comparable transaction prices) is almost never publicly available. The output must name these gaps explicitly and tell the operator what they need to request or investigate directly.

## Type 3 — Pre-signing check

**What it is:** The operator has chosen a location and is about to sign the lease. The location decision is made. The investigation is no longer about whether to proceed — it's about what the lease exposes them to and what the development pipeline looks like over the lease term.

**Diagnostic questions to ask first:**
- What's the lease term and what are the key review dates?
- Has the rent review mechanism been disclosed — is it CPI, fixed, or market?
- Is there a demolition clause or a permitted use clause?
- Have you had the lease reviewed by a solicitor?

**Why this matters:** This is the last point where research can change the outcome. The investigation is not re-litigating the location choice — it's identifying clause exposure and development risk that can still be mitigated before signing.

**Source priority for this mode:**
1. PlanningAlerts.org.au — the DA pipeline for the specific address and surrounding block over the lease term
2. CommercialRealEstate.com.au — comparable current listings to benchmark the rent against the market
3. Domain concepts (see domain-concepts.md) — ensure the operator understands what the key clauses mean before signing

**Output focus:** Development exposure (what's approved or pending that would affect the tenancy during the lease term), whether the stated rent is consistent with the market, and what the key lease clauses mean in practical terms. The recommendation not to sign without a solicitor's review belongs in every pre-signing output.

## Type 4 — Open scan

**What it is:** The operator has no specific location in mind. They're looking for where opportunities exist. The scope is broad.

**Diagnostic questions to ask first:**
- What's your concept and format?
- Is there any geography you're anchored to — a city, a region, somewhere you already know?
- What does "good opportunity" mean here — lowest cost to enter, underserved market, growth market?
- What have you already considered and ruled out?

**Why this matters:** "Anywhere" is not a researchable scope. An open scan must be narrowed through diagnostic questions before any search begins. The questions above usually reveal a starting anchor (a region the operator knows, a cost assumption that limits geography, a specific market gap they've observed). Investigate the anchor, not the continent.

**Source priority for this mode:**
Start with the diagnostic questions. Once geography is narrowed:
1. ABS QuickStats for shortlisted areas — establish baseline demographic comparison
2. CommercialRealEstate.com.au — compare lease rate environments across shortlisted areas
3. Café density sources — test the "underserved" hypothesis for each area

For regional markets, add:
4. Tourism Research Australia / council visitor economy data — test whether residential population understates trade potential

**Output focus:** A comparison across the shortlisted areas that surfaces where sources conflict between areas — not a recommendation of which area is best. The output identifies which areas warrant further investigation and what would need to be verified on the ground. The next step is always physical observation, not more desk research.

**Note on scope calibration:** If the operator cannot or will not narrow the geography through diagnostic questions, the investigation cannot proceed usefully. State this directly: "I can research specific locations once we've narrowed the scope. What geography are you anchored to?"
