# Domain Concepts

Key lease, planning, and market terms. When the researcher uses these correctly, it signals authenticity. When an operator uses them incorrectly, it signals a gap in their due diligence.

---

## Lease terms

**Make-good clause / make-good obligation**
The requirement to return the premises to its original condition at the end of the lease. In café fitouts, this typically means removing the kitchen, stripping custom joinery, patching walls, and sometimes reinstating the original floor. Make-good costs commonly run $30,000-$80,000 for a café fitout. Operators often don't realise the scale of this obligation until they're trying to exit. The make-good clause is one of the most important parts of any commercial lease and one of the least read.

**Permitted use clause**
Defines what the tenancy can be used for. A lease with a permitted use of "café and takeaway food" may prohibit alcohol sales, catering from the premises, or sub-leasing. A restrictive permitted use clause limits future flexibility. Always check whether the permitted use matches the intended operation — and what happens if the business concept changes.

**Demolition clause**
Allows the landlord to terminate the lease early if they intend to demolish or substantially redevelop the building. A demolition clause in a 5-year lease can effectively make it a shorter-term arrangement depending on the landlord's development intentions. Cross-reference with PlanningAlerts.org.au to check for lodged or approved DAs.

**Rent review mechanism**
How the rent changes during the lease term. Three types:
- CPI review — rent increases with the Consumer Price Index; most predictable and generally favourable for tenants in low-inflation environments
- Fixed review — rent increases by a fixed percentage (commonly 3-5% annually); predictable but may end up above or below market
- Market review — rent is reset to current market rate at review date; can be significantly higher or lower than the preceding rent; the least predictable and most risky for tenants in rising markets

The rent review mechanism is as important as the headline rent figure. A cheap headline rent with a market review clause in year 3 can become unviable.

**Rent-free period**
A period at the start of the lease during which no rent is payable, typically used to allow the tenant to fitout the space. A 3-month rent-free on a $60,000 pa lease is worth $15,000. When comparing listings, always establish the effective annual rent after the rent-free period ends.

**Fitout contribution / tenant incentive / landlord contribution**
A cash payment from the landlord to the tenant, typically to fund fitout costs. In competitive leasing markets, contributions of $30,000-$100,000 are common for café-sized spaces. Like rent-free periods, these reduce the effective cost of entry but end after year one. Asking whether a contribution is available is part of any lease negotiation.

**Holdover provisions**
What happens when a lease expires and the tenant stays without a new agreement in place. Typically converts to a month-by-month tenancy at the existing rent. Relevant when a lease is approaching expiry and renewal hasn't been agreed — a holdover period can be valuable (flexibility) or risky (landlord can terminate with short notice) depending on the circumstances.

**Outgoings**
Costs associated with the property that the tenant pays in addition to rent — typically council rates, water rates, building insurance, and common area maintenance in a shared building. The distinction between a gross lease (outgoings included in the stated rent) and a net lease (outgoings additional) can represent thousands of dollars in annual cost. Always establish whether the stated rent is gross or net.

**WIP fitout / warm shell / cold shell**
Describes how much of the fitout the tenancy already has when the tenant takes possession.
- Cold shell: bare concrete, no services, no amenities — the tenant builds everything from scratch; lowest rent, highest fitout cost
- Warm shell: services connected (power, plumbing, ventilation), no fitout; middle ground
- WIP (Work in Progress) fitout: a partial or previous fitout left in place by the outgoing tenant; may significantly reduce entry cost if it's useable for a café, or may be costly to remove if it isn't

## Planning terms

**DA (Development Application)**
A formal application to a local council for approval to carry out building or development works. Lodged before construction begins. DAs for significant residential or commercial development in a suburb are publicly searchable through PlanningAlerts.org.au and the relevant council portal. An approved DA on the same block as a prospective tenancy can signal: more foot traffic (residential development), a landlord with development intentions (demolition risk), or a change in strip character within the lease term.

**Planning permit**
In Victoria, the planning approval mechanism equivalent to a NSW DA. Both are searchable through PlanningAlerts.org.au.

**Planning overlay (heritage overlay, design overlay)**
Constraints on what can be done to a building or precinct, defined by the planning scheme. A heritage overlay may limit the extent of fitout works or signage. Check the relevant council planning map or planning scheme for any overlay that would affect the proposed use or fitout.

**Zoning**
Determines the permitted uses for a site. Commercial zones relevant to café tenancies:
- NSW: B1 Neighbourhood Centre, B2 Local Centre, B4 Mixed Use
- VIC: Commercial 1 Zone (formerly B2), Commercial 2 Zone (formerly B3)
Zoning information is available through the relevant council's planning portal. A retail food premises (café) is typically permissible in commercial zones but always verify — particularly for sites in mixed-use or transitional zones.

## Market terms

**Daytime population**
The number of people in a suburb during business hours — a combination of residents, commuting workers, students, and visitors. Differs significantly from residential population in employment hubs (daytime population >> residential), tourist destinations, and regional visitor economy markets. Relevant because café trade tracks daytime population more closely than residential population. Available through id.com.au.

**Passing trade vs. destination trade**
Passing trade: customers who come in because they walk past. Dependent on foot traffic volume and visibility — requires a high-pedestrian-count location.
Destination trade: customers who specifically seek out the venue. Less dependent on foot traffic, more dependent on reputation, concept quality, and category. A specialty coffee operator can build more destination trade than a generic café, which means location requirements are different — visibility matters less, a slightly off-pitch location matters less.

**Going concern**
A business sold as an operating entity — including the lease, equipment, fitout, brand, and customer relationships — as opposed to just the assets. A café sold as a going concern is priced partly on the business value (goodwill) and partly on the physical assets. The buyer takes on the existing lease.

**Goodwill**
The premium paid for a going concern above its physical asset value — representing the customer base, brand, and trading reputation. Goodwill is one of the most contested components of a café sale because it is the hardest to verify and the most likely to not transfer to a new operator.

**SDE (Seller's Discretionary Earnings)**
The net profit of a business before the owner's salary, interest, depreciation, and amortisation. The standard measure for small business valuation. Goodwill is typically expressed as a multiple of SDE. Always request the SDE figure when evaluating a going concern — "profitable" and "strong turnover" tell you nothing without it.

**Days on market**
How long a commercial space or going concern has been listed. Not visible on CommercialRealEstate.com.au listing cards — requires clicking through to individual listing pages. Long days on market (60+ days for a commercial lease in an active area) suggests either above-market asking terms or something specific about the property that the market is rejecting. This is one of the most useful data points available from public listing data.

**Comparable / comp**
A comparable transaction — another sale or lease in the same area and category that can be used to benchmark a price or rent. The challenge in Australian commercial real estate is that transaction prices are not publicly disclosed for small leases or small business sales. Comps must be assembled from: visible priced listings (asking prices only), conversations with local agents, or industry contacts who have transacted recently.

**Strip retail**
A row of ground-floor retail or hospitality tenancies on a street, typically in an inner-city or suburban main street context. Distinct from enclosed shopping centres. Strip retail foot traffic is driven by pedestrian flows on the street, which are influenced by parking, public transport, anchor tenants, and the density of surrounding activity.

**Anchor tenant**
A large or well-known retailer or institution whose presence drives foot traffic to a strip or centre — a supermarket, a post office, a pharmacy, a well-known hospitality operator. The presence and stability of anchor tenants significantly affects the viability of surrounding tenancies. Anchor departure (a supermarket relocating, a major employer closing) can substantially change the foot traffic dynamics of a strip.
