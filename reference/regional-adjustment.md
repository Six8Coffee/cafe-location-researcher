# Regional Adjustment Framework

Standard Australian location research frameworks are calibrated for metro markets. They systematically undervalue — and occasionally overvalue — regional markets where visitor economy drives trade more than residential population.

This framework exists because I opened in one of those markets. What the standard metrics predicted and what actually happened were different things.

---

## The core problem

ABS QuickStats gives you residential population. id.com.au gives you daytime population derived from the census. Both are built on who lives and works in a suburb.

For inner-city Melbourne or Sydney, that's a reasonable picture of who your customers are. For a regional market, it misses the most important variable: who passes through, who visits, and why.

A town of 8,000 residents on a major highway can serve 3,000 cups of coffee a week. A suburb of 40,000 residents in a major city's outer ring may support fewer. The standard metrics don't show you this. They show you the 8,000 and the 40,000 and leave you to draw the wrong conclusion.

---

## When to apply this framework

Apply the regional adjustment when any of the following are present:

**Highway adjacency.** Towns on major intercity highways (the Hume, the Federal, the Pacific, the New England, the Great Ocean Road) carry through-traffic that is often multiples of the residential population on a weekend. This traffic eats, drinks coffee, and stops for fuel. A café positioned for this traffic has a different catchment than the residential population suggests.

**Tourism destination.** Towns with significant holiday, weekend, or event-driven visitor economies (wine regions, ski towns, coastal holiday destinations, heritage towns on tourist routes) see trade that peaks well above what residential population predicts. The peak-to-trough variation also matters: a town that doubles its effective population on weekends but quiets significantly during the week has a different viability profile than a consistent metro catchment.

**Regional service centre.** Towns that function as the service centre for a large surrounding agricultural or rural catchment — bringing in workers, farmers, and families from surrounding areas for services, shopping, and food. The town's residential population understates its trade catchment.

**Event-driven.** Towns with major recurring events (agricultural shows, motorsport, music festivals, sporting fixtures) see concentrated periods of very high demand. Less relevant for daily trade viability but relevant for understanding peak capacity and awareness-building opportunity.

---

## What to search instead of (or in addition to) ABS

When visitor economy is likely to be material:

1. **Tourism Research Australia (tra.gov.au)** — national and state-level visitor data by region. Search for the relevant Statistical Area. Look for: total visitors per year, visitor nights, purpose of visit (holiday vs. business vs. visiting friends/family). Visitor nights is a better proxy for sustained trade than day visitor count.

2. **Local council tourism statistics** — many regional councils publish annual visitor economy reports or tourism dashboards. Search "[council name] visitor economy" or "[town] tourism report." Quality varies significantly: some councils produce detailed annual reports; others publish nothing.

3. **Highway traffic count data** — NSWDOT (Transport for NSW) and VicRoads publish Annual Average Daily Traffic (AADT) counts for major roads. A highway carrying 8,000-15,000 vehicles per day past a town represents a meaningful passing trade opportunity for a well-positioned, well-signed café.

4. **Local events calendar** — council websites, tourism boards, and local newspapers. A town hosting a major annual agricultural show may see 5-10x its residential population through its streets for 3-4 days. Useful for assessing whether awareness and trial opportunities exist beyond the daily trade.

---

## How to weight the adjustment

The visitor economy adjustment is not additive — you can't simply add visitor numbers to residential population and call it your catchment. Visitors spend differently, behave differently, and have different awareness of local operators than residents.

Useful rules of thumb:

- A café positioned on or immediately adjacent to a highway route (visible from the road, easy to park, clear signage) captures a higher proportion of through-traffic than one that requires local knowledge to find.
- Weekend and holiday visitor trade can sustain viability even when weekday residential trade is thin — but the inverse is also true: a café built around visitor trade is vulnerable to seasonal variation and event cancellation.
- Visitor economy data confirms potential, not certainty. It tells you the traffic exists. Physical observation on a representative weekend and a representative weekday tells you whether that traffic stops.

## The Yass example

Yass, NSW sits at the intersection of the Federal Highway and the Barton Highway — the primary route between Melbourne and Canberra, and between the South Coast and the ACT. The town's residential population is approximately 6,000. The standard demographic model would position it as a small regional market with limited hospitality viability.

What the model misses: on a typical weekend, traffic volumes through the town from both highways create a passing trade opportunity that is multiples of what the residential population predicts. The town functions as a rest stop, a fuel stop, and increasingly a destination in its own right for ACT residents seeking regional food and produce experiences.

The lesson: the demographic model is the floor, not the ceiling. In a visitor economy market, the ceiling is set by traffic, tourism, and positioning — and those require different sources to measure.

---

## What this framework cannot tell you

It can identify that a regional market likely has visitor economy contribution that standard metrics understate. It cannot quantify it precisely from desk research alone.

The sources above (TRA, council data, AADT counts) give you directional indicators. Converting those into reliable trade projections requires physical observation across representative periods — at minimum, a busy weekend, a quiet weekday, and one event period if applicable.

The adjustment framework reduces the risk of dismissing a viable regional market because the residential population looks thin. It does not replace the requirement to stand in the town and watch who is there.
