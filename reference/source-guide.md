# Source Guide

Every source the researcher uses — what it's good for, how to access it, and the specific reliability limitation for café location research.

No current market figures are stored here. Data ages. This file maps where to find current data and how to read what you find.

---

## ABS QuickStats

**What it's good for:** Residential demographic profile — population size, median age, median household income, median rent, employment by industry, educational attainment. The "employment by industry" breakdown is particularly useful: high % in cafés and restaurants signals both a target-demographic market and a competitive staffing environment.

**How to access:** abs.gov.au/census/find-census-data/quickstats → search by suburb name.

**Reliability limitation:** 2021 Census data — now five years old. Significant for suburbs that have changed rapidly (inner-city gentrification, new residential development, post-COVID demographic shift). Always flag the census year and cross-reference with current commercial activity to test whether the demographic profile remains predictive.

**What it cannot tell you:** Daytime population (use id.com.au), visitor economy contribution (use Tourism Research Australia or council data), or foot traffic at a specific site.

---

## id.com.au (community profiles)

**What it's good for:** Daytime population estimates — the number of people in a suburb during business hours, which differs significantly from residential population in employment hubs, tourist areas, and regional towns with visitor economies.

**How to access:** profile.id.com.au → search by council or suburb name → "workers" and "daytime population" data.

**Reliability limitation:** Derived from ABS data — carries the same 2021 lag. Most useful for establishing the relationship between residential and daytime population (a suburb where daytime population significantly exceeds residential population has an inflow workforce or visitor economy that standard metrics understate).

**What it cannot tell you:** Actual foot traffic past a specific tenancy.

## CommercialRealEstate.com.au

**What it's good for:** Active commercial lease listings — what's currently available in a given suburb, at what asking size and rate, and how many vacancies exist on a strip. The listing count and vacancy pattern are useful even when most listings say "Contact Agent."

**How to access:** commercialrealestate.com.au/for-lease → filter by suburb, select "Shops & Retail."

**Reliability limitation:** Asking prices only — not transaction prices. "Contact Agent" is standard for most Australian commercial listings, meaning the stated price (where visible) is an ask, not a market rate. The gap between asking and transacting can be significant in soft markets. Days-on-market is visible on individual listing pages (not on the listing card) — click through to find it.

**What it cannot tell you:** What deals are actually being done, what incentives (rent-free period, fitout contribution) are included in current transactions, or why any specific space is vacant.

## PlanningAlerts.org.au

**What it's good for:** Development application pipeline — every DA lodged with every Australian council, publicly searchable, no login required. The most consistently overlooked source in location research.

**How to access:** planningalerts.org.au → search by address or suburb. For a strip investigation, search the specific street address of any space being considered, plus the surrounding block. Browse mode surfaces a noisy mix of residential and commercial — search by address for useful results.

**Reliability limitation:** Shows applications as lodged and active. Historic approved DAs from 2+ years ago require searching the relevant council's own eServices or planning portal directly. Merri-bek (Brunswick/Collingwood), City of Sydney, and most metro councils have publicly accessible planning portals — accessibility varies for regional councils.

**What it cannot tell you:** Whether an approved DA will actually be built, or on what timeline.

---

## Bsale.com.au

**What it's good for:** Going concern listings — businesses for sale, including cafés. Useful for comparable asking prices, understanding what's on the market in a given area, and identifying the broker narrative conventions (what brokers in this market emphasise and what they omit).

**How to access:** bsale.com.au → search by business type and location.

**Reliability limitation:** Asking prices only — broker-represented. Actual sale prices for small business transactions are not publicly disclosed in Australia. Bsale listings systematically describe businesses in terms most favourable to the seller. The absence of certain information in a listing (no stated rent, no stated P&L detail) is itself a signal.

**What it cannot tell you:** Why a business is actually for sale, what the net profit is after owner wages, or what comparable businesses have actually sold for.

## Yelp / Urban List / Google Maps / local food media

**What it's good for:** Named café operators currently active in a suburb, strip character, editorial descriptions of a precinct's hospitality identity.

**How to access:** Search "[suburb] best cafes" across Yelp, Urban List, Broadsheet, and local council/tourism websites. Cross-reference to build a list of named operators.

**Reliability limitation:** Not comprehensive — misses new operators, low-profile operators, and operators who haven't accumulated reviews. Google Maps activity levels are a useful proxy for foot traffic patterns but not a substitute for physical observation. Editorial descriptions reflect the suburb's reputation at time of writing, which may lag reality.

**What it cannot tell you:** Whether existing operators are at capacity or quiet, their format and price point in detail, or their trade volumes.

## JLL / CBRE / Colliers / Knight Frank quarterly retail reports

**What it's good for:** Published $/sqm rent benchmarks for broad precincts (inner Melbourne, inner Sydney, etc.). The most credible published benchmark for commercial rents — provides independent context for evaluating individual listings.

**How to access:** jll.com.au, cbre.com.au, colliers.com.au — search "[city] retail market report." Most quarterly reports are available as PDFs with free email registration. The headline summaries are publicly visible; the suburb-level data is in the PDF.

**Reliability limitation:** Typically quarterly, not suburb-specific. Reports cover broad precincts rather than individual strips. The figures are for institutional-grade retail and may not reflect the market for smaller independent café spaces. Most useful as a cross-check against individual listing data — if listings are significantly above or below the benchmark, that gap is worth investigating.

**What it cannot tell you:** What actual deals are being done on a specific strip, or what incentives are included.

---

## Tourism Research Australia / local council visitor economy data

**What it's good for:** Visitor economy contribution in regional and tourist-destination markets — the gap between residential population and actual trade potential. Critical for any regional investigation where visitor economy drives a significant portion of hospitality trade.

**How to access:** tra.gov.au for national and state-level visitor data. Individual council websites often publish local tourism statistics — search "[council name] tourism statistics" or "[town] visitor numbers."

**Reliability limitation:** Variable quality across councils. Some publish detailed annual visitor economy reports; others publish nothing. National TRA data is comprehensive at state and regional level but not always suburb-specific.

**What it cannot tell you:** What proportion of visitors actually use hospitality (vs. passing through), or the day-of-week and seasonal distribution of visitor traffic.
