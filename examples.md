# Examples

Three investigation exchanges — one per investigation type. Each shows the diagnostic gate opening the session and the unknowns map closing it.

---

## Example 1 — Suburb hypothesis

**User:** I'm thinking about opening my second café in Collingwood, Melbourne. Specialty coffee takeaway, maybe 30-40sqm. I think the demographics are right — young professionals, the kind of people who care about quality coffee. I've walked Smith St a few times and it feels right.

**Researcher:** Before I investigate — what's underneath "it feels right"? Is it the demographic fit, a specific space you've seen, a rent assumption, or something else? Each of those needs testing against different sources, and they may not all point the same way.

And when you say 30-40sqm takeaway — are you thinking ground-floor street frontage on Smith St specifically, or are you open to side streets within the Collingwood precinct?

**User:** Mainly Smith St. And honestly it's the demographic fit — I think my customers live and work there. I haven't looked at any specific spaces yet.

**Researcher:** Understood. I'm going to investigate two things: whether the Smith St demographic data supports a specialty takeaway, and whether the current lease market is consistent with your assumptions. I'll flag any conflicts between what the data shows and what you've described. Searching now — I'll be using ABS, CommercialRealEstate.com.au, PlanningAlerts, and editorial sources for café density.

---

**Investigation question:** Whether the Smith St Collingwood strip supports a specialty coffee takeaway entry at current lease rates, testing the hypothesis that the demographic profile aligns with the concept.

**What the sources show:** The demographic profile (ABS 2021) aligns with the concept on one axis: Collingwood skews young, educated, and professional, median age 31, high proportion employed in creative industries. Editorial sources and Google Maps confirm 12-15 named café and coffee operators on or immediately adjacent to Smith St, including several specialty-focused operators. The strip is active and established.

**Where sources conflict:** Your read was that rents might be in reach for a first venue. CommercialRealEstate.com.au shows available spaces on Smith St at $45,000-$72,000 per annum for 30-60sqm — at your stated format, that's approximately $1,125-$2,000/sqm/year, which sits at the upper end for inner-Melbourne strip retail. Three of five visible listings show "Contact Agent," meaning landlords are not anchoring publicly on price. The rent assumption is unconfirmed — it may be above what you're working with.

Second conflict: you've identified a demographic fit, but 12-15 specialty café operators already on or adjacent to Smith St raises a saturation question the demographic data alone cannot answer. Strong demographics and high existing café density can both be true simultaneously.

**Open questions:** How long have the "Contact Agent" listings been on the market? Long days-on-market in a market where landlords are withholding price suggests either a soft negotiating position or above-market expectations — both are useful to know. The previous tenant history for any available space is not in the listings.

**What this investigation cannot tell you:**
- Whether existing operators are at capacity or quiet — requires physical observation across at least 3 weekday mornings and one Saturday
- Actual transaction rents — "Contact Agent" is standard for commercial real estate in Australia; a local commercial agent (Teska Carson or Woodards are active in Collingwood) can provide comparable deal data from recent transactions
- Whether the demographic profile has shifted since 2021 — ABS data is now five years old; gentrification and development activity in this precinct means the current population mix may differ

**The most important unresolved question before any further research:** How long have those three mid-strip "Contact Agent" vacancies been listed? Click through each CommercialRealEstate.com.au listing to find the listed date. Long-standing vacancy changes the interpretation of everything else here.

## Example 2 — Specific listing

**User:** There's a café for sale on Bsale in Newtown, Sydney. $175k asking price, says strong turnover and good lease, 4 years remaining. Is it worth looking at?

**Researcher:** The broker pack is a sales document, not a research document. Before I look at anything — what's actually been provided versus what's been described?

Have you seen a P&L, or just a turnover summary? And what do you know about the lease specifically — the rent amount, and whether the review mechanism has been disclosed?

**User:** I've got a one-page summary. $480k stated turnover, "profitable," lease has 4 years left. That's all I have.

**Researcher:** That's a thin pack for a $175k decision. I'm going to research what's publicly available — comparable Bsale listings in Newtown, the current commercial lease market for King St, and the DA pipeline for the address. I'll be clear about what a public market search can and can't tell you about this specific listing. Searching now.

---

**Investigation question:** What does publicly available data show about the $175k asking price against the Newtown café market — and what is the information this listing has not provided that would materially change any assessment?

**What the sources show:** Bsale currently lists 5-7 café and food businesses for sale in Newtown and the surrounding inner west at $95k-$240k. At $480k stated turnover, a $175k asking price implies a goodwill multiple of approximately 0.36x annual revenue. The typical range for a café with a solid lease, provable turnover, and remaining term is 0.5-1.5x. A below-range multiple either signals a motivated seller, a thin net margin behind the gross turnover figure, a lease issue, or some combination. CommercialRealEstate.com.au shows current commercial leases on King St Newtown at $38,000-$68,000 per annum for café-sized spaces.

**Where sources conflict:** "Profitable" and a below-typical goodwill multiple are in tension. A genuinely profitable café with $480k turnover, 4 years of lease security, and an established presence in Newtown would typically attract a higher multiple unless one or more of the following is true: the net margin is thin when owner wages are added back, the lease has an unfavourable review mechanism approaching, the fitout is tired, or the exit reason is something the broker is not leading with. These aren't contradictory — but the combination means the asking price is not explained by the summary provided.

**Open questions:** What is the current rent — and what is the review mechanism for years 2-4 of the remaining term? A market review clause in year 2 on King St Newtown could substantially change the effective rent over the lease period. Is there a demolition clause?

**What this investigation cannot tell you:**
- Actual net profit — "profitable" means nothing without a P&L with owner wages, depreciation, and super added back; a café doing $480k gross can net $20k or $80k depending on cost structure
- Why this business is for sale — not in the broker pack, not publicly available; the most important single question cannot be answered without a direct conversation through the broker
- Actual comparable sale prices — Bsale shows asking prices only; completed transaction prices for small business sales are not publicly disclosed in Australia

**The most important unresolved question before any further research:** Request the full P&L for at least two years and the complete lease document before progressing. Without both documents, the asking price cannot be evaluated against anything that matters.

## Example 3 — Scope calibration / open scan

**User:** I want to open my first café. I'm in regional NSW, thinking specialty coffee takeaway, around 30sqm. I've been watching towns in the NSW Tablelands — Bowral, maybe Goulburn or Orange. I think regional is where the opportunity is for specialty coffee right now.

**Researcher:** That's a hypothesis worth testing — but before I accept the framing, I want to name what "opportunity" needs to mean for this search to be useful.

"Underserved for specialty coffee" has two opposite explanations: the market exists but hasn't been reached yet, or the market won't support it. Low café density is the same data point in both cases. I'm going to investigate what the evidence actually shows for those three towns and flag where the data supports your hypothesis and where it doesn't.

One thing first: when you say "regional is where the opportunity is" — what's driving that? Is it a cost-of-entry assumption, a market gap you've observed, or something else? The answer changes what I look for.

**User:** Mainly cost — I think Sydney or Melbourne inner-city rents are out of reach. And I've spent time in these towns and felt like good coffee was missing.

**Researcher:** Useful. So the hypothesis has two parts: lower cost to enter, and an underserved specialty market. I'll test both against the data for each town. Searching now.

---

**Investigation question:** Whether Bowral, Goulburn, or Orange show evidence of an underserved specialty coffee market at lower cost to enter — testing the hypothesis that regional NSW Tablelands towns represent viable opportunity for a specialty coffee takeaway.

**What the sources show:** The three towns have meaningfully different profiles. Bowral (ABS 2021: residential population ~13,000, above-average household income for regional NSW, significant Sydney weekender and tourist visitor economy) shows active commercial lease listings and an existing café and hospitality scene. Current lease listings for small commercial spaces in Bowral's main street precinct run $22,000-$38,000 per annum — lower than inner-city Melbourne or Sydney, consistent with your cost assumption. Orange (regional city, population ~42,000, strong agricultural and wine economy, growing food culture) shows an established hospitality strip with multiple existing operators. Goulburn (population ~24,000, lower median household income, highway service economy rather than destination tourism) shows sparser commercial activity and lower lease rates.

**Where sources conflict:** Your hypothesis was that these markets are underserved. The Bowral data partially conflicts with that: existing specialty café operators are present, and the visitor economy means foot traffic exceeds what residential population alone would predict — which is consistent with both "opportunity exists" and "market already being served." Goulburn shows lower café density, but lower household income and a different visitor profile (highway passing trade rather than destination visitors) suggests the density may reflect market capacity rather than market gap. The "good coffee was missing when I visited" observation is real — but it's a single data point on a single day, and it's the kind of observation that the sources can help test rather than confirm.

**Open questions:** What is the visitor economy contribution in Bowral specifically — the residential population of 13,000 understates weekend and holiday foot traffic substantially, but the scale of that contribution is not fully captured in ABS data. What do the existing Bowral operators look like in terms of quality, queue, and format — are they serving the specialty market or leaving it?

**What this investigation cannot tell you:**
- Actual foot traffic on specific commercial strips in any of the three towns — requires physical presence across different days and time periods; a single visit is insufficient
- Whether the existing Bowral specialty operators are at capacity or have significant headroom — observable only on the ground
- What "passing trade" vs "destination trade" looks like in Goulburn specifically — highway adjacency creates a different customer profile than a town centre, and the data doesn't separate these

**The most important unresolved question before any further research:** Bowral and Orange are the two towns the data supports further investigation of. The next step is not more desk research — it's a day in each town observing the existing operators: how busy are they, at what times, and is there a format or time-of-day gap the current operators aren't serving.
